What are Plant Tissues?

Plant tissues are groups of cells that work together to perform specific functions in plants.

Kinds of Plant Tissues:

1. Meristematic Tissues

These are actively dividing cells that allow the plant to grow. They are usually small, have thin walls, and contain dense cytoplasm with a large nucleus.

2. Permanent Tissues

These are mature tissues whose cells have stopped dividing and now perform specific functions. Permanent tissues are further divided into simple and complex tissues.

  • Simple tissues: made of one type of cell.
  • Complex tissues: made of multiple cell types.

What is a Muscle Tissue?

It is a type of animal tissue that allows movement. It’s made
of cells called muscle fibers that can contract and relax.

Three types of Muscle Tissue:

Skeletal muscle - attached to bones, voluntary movement,
striated.

Cardiac muscle - heart muscles, involuntary, striated, pumps
blood.

Smooth muscle - walls of organs (like the stomach and
intestines), involuntary, not striated.

What is the Nervous Tissue?

It is the tissue in animals that senses stimuli and transmits
signals throughout the body. It’s made of neurons and
supporting neuroglial cells.

Two main cell types of the Nervous Tissue:

Neurons - the functional units that carry electrical signals.
Each neuron has:
• Cell body (soma): contains the nucleus and organelles.
• Dendrites: short branches that receive signals from other
  neurons.
• Axon: long fiber that transmits impulses to other neurons,
  muscles, or glands.

Neuroglia - support cells that protect, nourish, and maintain
neurons. They also help in repairing nervous tissue and
maintaining the environment around neurons.

What are Measurements?

The determination of the size or quantity of something. Every measurement consists of a number and a unit. They are essential in science and daily life (cooking, commerce, clothing). Measurement standards and systems vary by region and context.

Accuracy and Precision

Accuracy – how close a measurement is to the true value.

Precision – how close repeated measurements are to each other.

  • Accurate but not precise: values centered on true value but scattered.
  • Precise but not accurate: values tightly clustered but offset (systematic error).
  • Accurate and precise: correct and consistent measurements.
  • Neither: scattered and incorrect values.

Systems of Measurement

English System – uses unrelated conversion factors (e.g., 12 in = 1 ft).

Metric System – uses base-ten units; easier conversions.

SI System – modern metric system used in science.

  • Base units: meter (m), kilogram (kg), second (s), etc.
  • Derived units: m³ (volume), m/s (speed), etc.
Click this link for more info

Metric Prefixes

Prefixes indicate large or small multiples of a unit.

Kilo (k) – 10³
Centi (c) – 10⁻²
Milli (m) – 10⁻³
Micro (µ) – 10⁻⁶

Unit Conversion (Dimensional Analysis)

Multiply by conversion factors (fractions equal to 1) to cancel units and obtain the desired unit.

For derived units like area or volume, remember:
• Area conversions are squared.
• Volume conversions are cubed.

Example: 1 m³ = (100 cm)³ = 1,000,000 cm³

Significant Figures

Exact numbers – infinite sig figs (definitions, counting).

Inexact numbers – limited sig figs (measurements).

Rules:
• Non-zero digits are significant.
• Leading zeros are NOT significant.
• Captive zeros are significant.
• Trailing zeros are significant only if a decimal is present.
• Use scientific notation to remove ambiguity.

Density

Density = mass ÷ volume.

An object floats if its density is less than the fluid; sinks if greater.

Water density ≈ 1 g/cm³ or 1000 kg/m³.
