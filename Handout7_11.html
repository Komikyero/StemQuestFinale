How a Cell Knows What Cell to Become

Cells rely on DNA as the blueprint for structure and function. DNA condenses into chromosomes through:

  • DNA wraps around histones → nucleosomes
  • Nucleosomes coil → chromatin fibers
  • Chromatin condenses → chromatids
  • Two chromatids join at centromere → chromosome
The Cell Cycle

The cell cycle is an ordered sequence of growth and division consisting of:

  • Interphase — G0, G1, S, G2
  • Mitotic Phase — Prophase, Metaphase, Anaphase, Telophase, Cytokinesis
Interphase

The longest and most active part of the cell cycle.

  • G0 — cell rests; normal metabolic activity
  • G1 — cell grows and synthesizes materials
  • S Phase — DNA replication; centrosomes duplicate
  • G2 — organelles reproduce; preparation for mitosis
Mitotic Phase

Cell divides its nucleus and cytoplasm to form two daughter cells.

  • Prophase — chromosomes condense; spindle forms
  • Metaphase — chromosomes line up at the equator
  • Anaphase — sister chromatids separate
  • Telophase — nuclear membranes reform
  • Cytokinesis — cytoplasm divides
Cell Cycle Checkpoints
  • G1 Checkpoint — cell size, nutrients, growth factors
  • G2 Checkpoint — DNA replication accuracy
  • M Checkpoint — spindle attachment
Diversity in Individuals

Organisms contain two major cell types:

  • Somatic cells — diploid (2n)
  • Gametes — haploid (n)

Diversity increases through the combination of gametes from two parents.

Meiosis Overview

Meiosis reduces chromosome number from diploid to haploid and produces gametes.

It consists of Meiosis I and Meiosis II.

Meiosis I — Unique Processes
  • Synapsis — homologous chromosomes pair
  • Crossing-over — exchange of genetic material
  • Tetrad formation — homologs connect at chiasmata
  • Independent assortment — random orientation
  • Produces two haploid daughter cells
Meiosis II
  • Chromosomes re-condense
  • Sister chromatids separate
  • Results in four genetically unique haploid cells
