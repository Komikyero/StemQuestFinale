What is ATP?

ATP (Adenosine Triphosphate) is the cell’s energy currency. It has three phosphate groups, ribose sugar, and an adenine base. Its phosphate bonds store high energy due to repelling charges.

ATP–ADP Cycle

Phosphorylation releases energy (exergonic).
Dephosphorylation requires energy (endergonic).

The ATP–ADP cycle regenerates ATP using energy from cellular processes.

Metabolic Pathways

Sequence of chemical reactions inside cells that require enzymes and manage energy and building blocks.

Cellular Respiration
  • Glycolysis — Cytoplasm
  • Krebs Cycle — Mitochondrial matrix
  • Electron Transport Chain — Inner mitochondrial membrane
Glycolysis

Net products: 2 ATP (net), 2 NADH, and 2 pyruvate molecules.

Krebs Cycle

Produces CO₂, ATP, NADH, and FADH₂. Molecules are recycled throughout the cycle.

ETC & Chemiosmosis

NADH and FADH₂ donate electrons; ETC releases energy gradually. Final electron acceptor: oxygen.

Anaerobic Respiration

When O₂ is absent, cells perform fermentation: lactic acid in muscles or alcohol in brewing.

Organizational Chart of Matter

Matter: pure substances (elements, compounds) and mixtures. Compounds can be molecular or ionic.

Chemical Equations & Reactions

Reactants → Products. Use state symbols (s, l, g, aq). Watch capitalization (Ni ≠ NI). Coefficients show amounts; subscripts show composition.

Types of Reactions

Synthesis A + B → AB.
Decomposition AB → A + B.
Displacement A + BC → AC + B.
Redox — electron transfer (oxidation/reduction).

Common Polyatomic Ions
  • Phosphate — PO₄³⁻
  • Carbonate — CO₃²⁻
  • Sulfate — SO₄²⁻
  • Nitrate — NO₃⁻
  • Hydroxide — OH⁻
  • Acetate — C₂H₃O₂⁻
  • Ammonium — NH₄⁺
  • Chromate — CrO₄²⁻
  • Permanganate — MnO₄⁻

Writing Formulas & Nomenclature

For ionic compounds: swap charges for subscripts (use parentheses for polyatomic ions). Binary molecular: use prefixes + element names + -ide. Type II metals: put charge as Roman numeral.

Greek Prefixes & Oxyanion Rules
  • Mono-, Di-, Tri-, Tetra-, Penta-, Hexa-, Hepta-, Octa-, Nona-, Deca-
  • Oxyanion suffixes: -ite (less O), -ate (more O); prefixes: hypo- (less), per- (more)

What Are Gases?

Gases expand to fill containers, have low density, are compressible, and have particles moving rapidly in all directions. Their behavior led to the foundation of modern chemistry.

Boyle’s Law

Pressure ∝ 1/Volume (T and n constant)

Formula: P₁V₁ = P₂V₂

As volume decreases, pressure increases.

Charles’s Law

Volume ∝ Temperature (P and n constant)

Formula: V₁/T₁ = V₂/T₂

Heating a gas increases its volume.

Gay-Lussac’s Law

Pressure ∝ Temperature (V and n constant)

Formula: P₁/T₁ = P₂/T₂

Higher temperature increases particle collisions, raising pressure.

Avogadro’s Law

Volume ∝ moles of gas (P and T constant)

Formula: V₁/n₁ = V₂/n₂

More moles = larger volume.

Ideal Gas Law

PV = nRT

Combines all gas laws into one relationship.

  • P – pressure
  • V – volume
  • n – moles
  • T – temperature (K)
  • R – gas constant
STP Conditions

Standard Temperature and Pressure:

  • 0°C (273 K)
  • 1 atm
  • 1 mole of gas = 22.4 L
Combined Gas Law

(P₁V₁)/(T₁) = (P₂V₂)/(T₂)

Used when multiple conditions change.

Dalton’s Law of Partial Pressures

Total pressure = sum of all individual gas pressures.

Formula: Ptotal = P₁ + P₂ + P₃ + …

Gas Stoichiometry

Use PV = nRT to convert between gas volume and moles in reactions.

Strategy:

  • Use balanced equation to find moles.
  • Use Ideal Gas Law for volume or pressure.
Kinetic Molecular Theory (KMT)

Explains gas behavior using particle motion:

  • Particles have negligible volume.
  • No attractive/repulsive forces.
  • Constant, random, straight-line motion.
  • Collisions are perfectly elastic.
  • Average kinetic energy ∝ temperature (K).
Kinetic Energy Formula

KEavg = (3/2) kB T

As temperature increases, particle motion and pressure also increase.
